# Moral Arguments — Deep Defense Library

> Arguments from the existence of objective moral values and duties to God as the foundation of morality. From mainstream Trinitarian Christian apologists and philosophers.

---

## 1. The Moral Argument (William Lane Craig's Formulation)

**Core Formulation:**
1. If God does not exist, objective moral values and duties do not exist.
2. Objective moral values and duties do exist.
3. Therefore, God exists.

### Deep Defense of Premise 1: Without God, No Objective Morality

#### A. The Naturalistic Problem
On atheistic naturalism, human beings are the accidental byproducts of a blind, purposeless process — matter in motion governed by impersonal physical laws. On this view:

- **We are biological machines.** Our moral feelings are neurochemical states — patterns of neuronal activity shaped by natural selection for survival value, not for truth-tracking. As **J.L. Mackie** (an atheist) acknowledged: "Moral properties constitute so odd a cluster of qualities and relations that they are most unlikely to have arisen in the ordinary course of events, without an all-powerful God to create them" (*The Miracle of Theism*, 1982, p. 115).

- **Moral obligations require a moral authority.** An obligation is something you *owe* to someone. On atheism, there is no transcendent authority to whom we owe moral duties. Moral "oughts" reduce to social conventions, personal preferences, or evolutionary programming — none of which are genuinely binding.

- **The "is-ought" problem (Hume).** You cannot derive moral prescriptions ("ought") from mere descriptions ("is") of the natural world. Natural selection tells us what *is* (we have certain moral instincts), not what *ought* to be. The fact that humans evolved to feel empathy does not mean empathy is objectively good — just as the fact that humans evolved aggressive instincts does not mean aggression is objectively good.

#### B. The Failure of Secular Moral Theories

**i. Utilitarianism (Bentham, Mill)**
Utilitarianism says "maximize happiness/well-being." But: Why should we maximize happiness? This is itself a moral claim that needs justification. Furthermore, utilitarianism can justify horrific acts (torturing one person to save many) — revealing that it does not capture our deepest moral intuitions.

**ii. Kantian Ethics**
Kant's Categorical Imperative ("Act only according to that maxim by which you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law") provides a formal test for moral rules but no ontological grounding. *Why* should we act according to universalizable maxims? Kant himself recognized this and posited God as a necessary postulate of practical reason.

**iii. Social Contract Theory (Hobbes, Rawls)**
Morality as a human social contract makes moral obligations relative to specific societies. On this view, slavery was "moral" in societies that endorsed it. This is intolerable — we believe slavery was objectively wrong even when societies endorsed it. Social contract theory cannot ground objective morality.

**iv. Evolutionary Ethics**
If morality is just an evolved survival strategy, then it has no objective truth-value. As **Michael Ruse** (an atheist philosopher of biology) writes: "Morality is just an aid to survival and reproduction... and any deeper meaning is illusory" (*Taking Darwin Seriously*, 1986, p. 253). On this view, morality is a useful fiction — not a genuine feature of reality.

**v. Sam Harris's "Moral Landscape"**
Sam Harris argues that science can determine moral values by measuring human well-being. But: (a) "Well-being" is itself a value-laden concept — defining it involves making moral judgments. (b) Why should we value well-being? This is a philosophical question, not a scientific one. (c) Harris's project presupposes moral realism — the very thing that needs grounding.

#### C. The Theistic Foundation
On theism, objective moral values are grounded in God's nature. God is not merely a being who happens to be good — He *is* goodness itself. Moral values are reflections of God's character:
- **Love** — because God is love (1 John 4:8)
- **Justice** — because God is just (Deuteronomy 32:4)
- **Truthfulness** — because God cannot lie (Titus 1:2)
- **Holiness** — because God is holy (Isaiah 6:3)

Moral duties (obligations) are grounded in God's commands, which necessarily flow from His nature. This is the **Divine Nature Theory** (Robert Adams, *Finite and Infinite Goods*, 1999) — also called Modified Divine Command Theory.

**Key Defenders:**
- **C.S. Lewis** — *Mere Christianity* (1952), *The Abolition of Man* (1943)
- **William Lane Craig** — *Reasonable Faith* (2008), ch. 4; numerous debates
- **Robert Adams** — *Finite and Infinite Goods* (1999)
- **J.P. Moreland** — *Scaling the Secular City* (1987), ch. 5
- **Paul Copan** — *A Good God* (2011)

### Deep Defense of Premise 2: Objective Moral Values DO Exist

#### A. Moral Experience
We apprehend objective moral truths with the same immediacy and certainty as mathematical or logical truths. Consider:
- **It is objectively wrong to torture an innocent child for fun.** This is not a cultural preference; it is true in every possible world, in every culture, at all times.
- **The Holocaust was objectively evil.** Not merely "distasteful" or "socially disadvantageous" — but genuinely, objectively wicked.
- **Human beings have intrinsic dignity and worth.** Slavery, rape, and genocide violate something real — not merely cultural norms.

As **C.S. Lewis** argued: "Whenever you find a man who says he does not believe in a real Right and Wrong, you will find the same man going back on this a moment later. He may break his promise to you, but if you try breaking one to him he will be complaining 'It's not fair' before you can say Jack Robinson" (*Mere Christianity*, 1952, pp. 5-6).

#### B. The Self-Defeating Nature of Moral Relativism
If moral relativism is true, then:
- It is not *objectively wrong* to be morally intolerant — undermining the relativist's own commitment to tolerance.
- Moral progress is impossible — if morality is relative, then abolishing slavery was not "progress" but merely "change."
- Moral reformers (Wilberforce, MLK, Bonhoeffer) were not *right* — they simply had different preferences from their contemporaries.
- These conclusions are absurd. Therefore, moral relativism is false.

#### C. Universal Moral Intuitions
Despite cultural variation in moral codes, all cultures share core moral convictions: prohibitions against murder, theft, and lying; duties of care for children; values of courage, justice, and generosity. This universality is best explained by objective moral reality, not by cultural convergence.

**C.S. Lewis** documented this in the Appendix to *The Abolition of Man* (1943), compiling parallel moral principles from Ancient Egyptian, Babylonian, Hindu, Chinese, Greek, Roman, Norse, and Aboriginal cultures — calling it "The Tao" (the universal moral law).

## 2. Responding to the Euthyphro Dilemma

**The Dilemma (Plato, *Euthyphro*):**
Is something good because God commands it? Or does God command it because it is good?

- **Horn 1 (Divine Command Theory — crude version):** If something is good merely because God commands it, then morality is arbitrary — God could command murder, rape, or cruelty and they would be "good."
- **Horn 2:** If God commands things because they are independently good, then goodness is independent of God — God is irrelevant to morality.

### The Solution: The Third Option (Divine Nature Theory)

The Euthyphro Dilemma presents a *false* dichotomy. The correct answer is a third option:

**God's commands are neither arbitrary nor derived from an external standard. God's nature IS the standard of goodness.**

God is essentially loving, just, holy, faithful, and good. His commands necessarily flow from His nature — He cannot command evil because He cannot act against His own nature. Morality is grounded in the unchanging character of God.

- God does not command love arbitrarily — He commands love because He IS love.
- God does not consult an external moral rulebook — He IS the rulebook.
- God's nature provides the fixed, unchanging standard that grounds all objective morality.

As **Robert Adams** argues: "The Good is God's nature. God's commands are expressions of His nature, not arbitrary fiats" (*Finite and Infinite Goods*, 1999).

**Key Defenders:**
- **Thomas Aquinas** — God's goodness is identical with His being (*Summa Theologiae* I, q.6).
- **William Lane Craig** — "The Euthyphro Dilemma is a false dilemma. There is a third option: God's nature is the Good" (*Reasonable Faith*, 2008).
- **Robert Adams** — *Finite and Infinite Goods* (1999): Formal philosophical defense.
- **William Alston** — "What Euthyphro Should Have Said" (1990).

## 3. The Argument from Conscience (John Henry Newman)

**Formulation:**
1. Conscience presents itself as an authority — a voice of command that we did not create and cannot silence.
2. Conscience evokes feelings of guilt, shame, and fear — directed at a *person*, not an abstract principle.
3. The best explanation for conscience is that it is the voice of God — a moral lawgiver to whom we are accountable.

**John Henry Newman's Defense:**
"If, as is the case, we feel responsibility, are ashamed, are frightened, at transgressing the voice of conscience, this implies that there is One to whom we are responsible, before whom we are ashamed, whose claims upon us we fear. If the natural sense of conscience is to be explained by the hypothesis of a God, this is a proof, as far as it goes, of the Being of God" (*Grammar of Assent*, 1870).

## 4. The Argument from Human Dignity and Rights

**Formulation:**
1. Human rights and human dignity are foundational to modern civilization (Universal Declaration of Human Rights, 1948).
2. Human rights require grounding — why do humans have inherent dignity?
3. On atheistic naturalism, humans are complex arrangements of matter with no intrinsic value different from rocks or bacteria.
4. Only theism grounds genuine human dignity — humans are made in the image of God (*imago Dei*, Genesis 1:27).

**Defense:**
The entire tradition of human rights is historically rooted in Christian theology. The concept of every human being as bearing the image of God — regardless of race, sex, intelligence, productivity, or social status — is the ultimate foundation for universal human dignity.

**Key Defenders:**
- **Nicholas Wolterstorff** — *Justice: Rights and Wrongs* (2008): Argues that only theism can ground human rights.
- **Jürgen Habermas** (an atheist) — Acknowledges: "Christianity, and nothing else, is the ultimate foundation of liberty, conscience, human rights, and democracy... We continue to nourish ourselves from this source" (2006 speech, "Pre-political Moral Foundations of a Free Republic").

## 5. The Argument from Evil (Turned on its Head)

**Formulation (C.S. Lewis):**
1. The atheist argues that evil disproves God.
2. But to call something "evil" presupposes an objective moral standard.
3. An objective moral standard presupposes God.
4. Therefore, the very argument from evil against God actually presupposes God's existence.

**C.S. Lewis's Experience:**
"My argument against God was that the universe seemed so cruel and unjust. But how had I got this idea of just and unjust? A man does not call a line crooked unless he has some idea of a straight line. What was I comparing this universe with when I called it unjust? ... Thus in the very act of trying to prove that God did not exist — in other words, that the whole of reality was senseless — I found I was forced to assume that one part of reality — namely my idea of justice — was full of sense" (*Mere Christianity*, 1952, pp. 38-39).

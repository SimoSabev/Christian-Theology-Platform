# Historical Evidence — Deep Defense Library

> Arguments from historical evidence for the truth of Christianity — the Resurrection, reliability of the New Testament, early Church testimony. From mainstream Trinitarian Christian historians, theologians, and apologists.

---

## 1. The Minimal Facts Argument for the Resurrection

**Core Formulation (Gary Habermas & Michael Licona):**

Using ONLY facts that meet two criteria: (a) supported by strong historical evidence, and (b) accepted by the vast majority of scholars who study the subject (including skeptics and non-Christians):

### The Minimal Facts

#### Fact 1: Jesus Died by Crucifixion
**Evidence:**
- **Multiple independent sources:** All four Gospels (Mark 15, Matthew 27, Luke 23, John 19), Paul (1 Corinthians 15:3, Philippians 2:8), Acts, 1 Peter.
- **Non-Christian sources:** Tacitus (*Annals* 15.44, c. 116 AD): "Christ, from whom the name had its origin, suffered the extreme penalty during the reign of Tiberius at the hands of one of our procurators, Pontius Pilate." Josephus (*Antiquities* 18.3.3, c. 93 AD): "Pilate, upon hearing him accused by men of the highest standing amongst us, had condemned him to be crucified." Lucian of Samosata (*The Death of Peregrinus*, c. 165 AD) refers to Christ as "the man who was crucified in Palestine."
- **Medical analysis:** The Journal of the American Medical Association published an analysis concluding: "Clearly, the weight of historical and medical evidence indicates that Jesus was dead before the wound to his side was inflicted" (JAMA, March 1986).
- **Scholarly consensus:** Virtually 100% of scholars who study the historical Jesus accept his death by crucifixion. Even the highly skeptical Jesus Seminar accepted this as historical.

**John Dominic Crossan** (skeptic, co-founder of the Jesus Seminar): "That he was crucified is as sure as anything historical can ever be" (*Jesus: A Revolutionary Biography*, 1994, p. 145).

#### Fact 2: The Disciples Had Experiences They Believed Were Appearances of the Risen Jesus
**Evidence:**
- **Paul's testimony (1 Corinthians 15:3-8):** This creed is dated by virtually all scholars to within 2-5 years of the crucifixion (c. 35-38 AD). Paul received it from the Jerusalem apostles (Galatians 1:18-19). It lists appearances to Peter, the Twelve, 500 brothers at once, James, all the apostles, and Paul himself. This is the earliest evidence for the Resurrection — not a later legend but a creedal formula traceable to the first eyewitnesses.
- **Multiple independent sources:** The appearances are attested in Paul, Mark, the M source (Matthew's special material), the L source (Luke's special material), John, and Acts — meeting the criterion of multiple attestation.
- **Transformation of the disciples:** The disciples were transformed from fearful, hiding fugitives (Mark 14:50, John 20:19) into bold proclaimers willing to suffer and die for their testimony that Jesus had risen. This transformation demands explanation.
- **Scholarly consensus:** Gary Habermas's survey of over 3,400 academic publications on the Resurrection (1975-2003) found that approximately 75% of scholars — including skeptics — accept the historicity of the disciples' experiences.

**Gerd Lüdemann** (skeptic, atheist NT scholar): "It may be taken as historically certain that Peter and the disciples had experiences after Jesus's death in which Jesus appeared to them as the risen Christ" (*What Really Happened to Jesus*, 1995, p. 80).

#### Fact 3: The Persecutor Paul Was Suddenly Converted
**Evidence:**
- **Paul's own testimony:** Galatians 1:13-16, 1 Corinthians 15:8-10, Philippians 3:4-8. Paul was a zealous Pharisee who violently persecuted the early church. His sudden conversion to the faith he was destroying is historically indisputable.
- **Paul had nothing to gain and everything to lose.** He was a rising star in Pharisaic Judaism (Galatians 1:14). His conversion brought him imprisonment, beatings, stoning, shipwreck, and eventually execution (2 Corinthians 11:23-27). He gained nothing worldly.
- **His conversion cannot be explained by prior psychological disposition.** He was not sympathetic, grief-stricken, or predisposed to believe. He was actively hostile. This rules out theories of wish-fulfillment, cognitive dissonance, or grief hallucination.

#### Fact 4: The Skeptic James (Jesus's Brother) Was Suddenly Converted
**Evidence:**
- **Jesus's brothers did not believe during his ministry** (Mark 3:21, 31-35; John 7:5). This is accepted as historical because it meets the criterion of embarrassment — the early Church would not invent the detail that Jesus's own family thought he was out of his mind.
- **James became a leader of the Jerusalem church** (Acts 15, Galatians 2:9) and was executed for his faith (Josephus, *Antiquities* 20.9.1, c. 62 AD).
- **What transformed a skeptic into a martyr?** The best explanation: James saw the risen Jesus (1 Corinthians 15:7).

#### Fact 5: The Tomb Was Found Empty
**Evidence:**
- **The Jerusalem Factor:** Christianity began in Jerusalem — the very city where Jesus was publicly executed and buried. If the tomb were not empty, the Jewish authorities could have simply produced the body and killed the movement instantly. They never did.
- **Enemy attestation:** The Jewish authorities did not deny the empty tomb — they claimed the disciples stole the body (Matthew 28:11-15). This tacitly admits the tomb was empty. This polemic is attested in Justin Martyr's *Dialogue with Trypho* (c. 160 AD) and in the Toledot Yeshu tradition.
- **Women as first witnesses:** All four Gospels report women as the first witnesses of the empty tomb. In first-century Jewish and Greco-Roman culture, women's testimony was not admissible in court (Josephus, *Antiquities* 4.8.15). If the account were invented, male witnesses would have been named. The embarrassing detail of female witnesses powerfully supports historicity (criterion of embarrassment).
- **The "he was raised" formula:** The consistent early Christian formula "he was buried... he was raised" (1 Corinthians 15:4) presupposes an empty tomb — what was buried is what was raised.

### Refuting Alternative Explanations

#### A. The Hallucination Theory
**Claim:** The disciples experienced grief hallucinations.

**Refutation:**
1. **Hallucinations are individual, not group phenomena.** Hallucinations are private psychological experiences tied to individual brain states. "Group hallucinations" are unknown to modern psychology. The appearance to the 500 (1 Corinthians 15:6) and to the Twelve cannot be hallucinations.
2. **Paul was not grief-stricken but hostile.** He was not psychologically predisposed to "see" Jesus — he was trying to destroy Christianity.
3. **James was a skeptic.** He did not believe in Jesus during his ministry — he was not in a state of grief or religious expectation.
4. **Hallucinations cannot explain the empty tomb.** Even if the disciples hallucinated, the body would still be in the tomb.
5. **Jewish theology had no expectation of a single individual rising before the end of time.** The disciples had no framework for expecting or imagining a pre-eschaton resurrection. In Jewish belief, resurrection was corporate (all the righteous together) and eschatological (at the end of history). The disciples would have honored Jesus as a martyred prophet, not proclaimed a resurrection.

#### B. The Conspiracy Theory (Disciples Stole the Body)
**Claim:** The disciples fabricated the Resurrection.

**Refutation:**
1. **People die for what they believe is true, not for what they know is a lie.** The disciples willingly suffered and died for their testimony. Peter was crucified upside down, James son of Zebedee was beheaded (Acts 12:2), Paul was executed in Rome. While people do die for false beliefs, they do not knowingly die for fabrications.
2. **The transformation of the disciples is unexplained.** Liars do not make martyrs. The disciples showed every sign of genuine conviction, not calculated deception.
3. **The conspiracy would have had to involve too many people.** Paul lists over 500 witnesses (1 Corinthians 15:6) — a conspiracy of this magnitude would inevitably collapse.

#### C. The Swoon Theory (Jesus Survived Crucifixion)
**Claim:** Jesus did not actually die — he merely fainted and later revived.

**Refutation:**
1. **Roman soldiers were professional executioners.** They confirmed death before releasing a body. The spear thrust to the side (John 19:34) — producing "blood and water" (separation of blood and serum in a dead body) — is a medically consistent detail.
2. **A barely-alive Jesus emerging from the tomb would not have inspired worship.** A man who had been scourged, crucified, and entombed for three days — if he somehow survived — would have been a pathetic figure in need of medical attention, not a glorified conqueror of death.
3. **David Friedrich Strauss** (a 19th-century skeptic who denied the Resurrection) wrote: "It is impossible that a being who had stolen half-dead out of the sepulchre... could have given to the disciples the impression that he was a Conqueror over death and the grave, the Prince of Life" (*A New Life of Jesus*, 1865).

#### D. The Legend/Myth Theory
**Claim:** The Resurrection stories are legends that developed over decades or centuries.

**Refutation:**
1. **The 1 Corinthians 15 creed dates to within 2-5 years of the crucifixion.** This is far too early for legendary development. Legends require generations to develop — when eyewitnesses are still alive to contradict false claims, myths cannot take root.
2. **A.N. Sherwin-White** (Oxford historian of Roman history) demonstrated that the rate of legendary development in the ancient world required at least two full generations. The Resurrection proclamation began immediately (*Roman Society and Roman Law in the New Testament*, 1963).
3. **The Resurrection narratives lack mythological features.** Compare the canonical Gospels with actual mythological texts (the Gospel of Peter, which describes a cross walking out of the tomb and reaching to heaven) — the canonical accounts are restrained, realistic, and include embarrassing details (women witnesses, disciples' doubt, Peter's denial).
4. **N.T. Wright's argument from mutation:** The early Christians believed in bodily resurrection — a belief with no precedent in Jewish or pagan thought. Jewish theology expected corporate eschatological resurrection. Pagan thought denied bodily resurrection entirely. Something happened to cause this unprecedented mutation in the concept of resurrection — and the only sufficient cause is the Resurrection itself (*The Resurrection of the Son of God*, 2003).

## 2. The Reliability of the New Testament

### A. Manuscript Evidence
The New Testament is the best-attested document from antiquity — by an enormous margin:

| Document | Earliest Copy | Time Gap | Number of Copies |
|----------|--------------|----------|-----------------|
| **New Testament** | **c. 125 AD (P52)** | **~25-50 years** | **~5,800+ Greek MSS** |
| Homer's Iliad | c. 400 BC | ~500 years | ~1,900 |
| Caesar's Gallic Wars | c. 900 AD | ~950 years | ~10 |
| Plato's dialogues | c. 900 AD | ~1,200 years | ~7 |
| Tacitus's Annals | c. 1100 AD | ~1,000 years | ~2 |

**F.F. Bruce** wrote: "There is no body of ancient literature in the world which enjoys such a wealth of good textual attestation as the New Testament" (*The New Testament Documents: Are They Reliable?*, 1960).

### B. Early Dating
- **Paul's letters (c. 48-64 AD):** Written within 15-30 years of Jesus's death. 1 Thessalonians (c. 50 AD) is often considered the earliest NT document.
- **The Synoptic Gospels:** Mark (c. 55-70 AD), Matthew (c. 60-80 AD), Luke (c. 60-80 AD). Acts ends abruptly with Paul under house arrest in Rome (c. 62 AD), suggesting Luke-Acts was written before Paul's execution — placing Luke before 62 AD and Mark even earlier.
- **The 1 Corinthians 15 creed (c. 35-38 AD):** This is the earliest testimony to the Resurrection — traceable to the first Christians within years of the events.

### C. Eyewitness Testimony
**Richard Bauckham** (*Jesus and the Eyewitnesses*, 2006) argues that the Gospels are based on eyewitness testimony:
- The Gospels name specific individuals as sources (Peter in Mark, the Beloved Disciple in John).
- The practice of "inclusio" — framing a narrative between references to an eyewitness — was a recognized historiographical technique.
- The Gospels employ personal names in patterns consistent with Palestinian Jewish naming practices of the first century — demonstrating access to genuine local knowledge.

### D. Archaeological Confirmation
Numerous details in the Gospels and Acts have been confirmed by archaeology:
- **The Pool of Bethesda** (John 5:2) — discovered in the 19th century with five porticoes exactly as described.
- **The Pool of Siloam** (John 9:7) — discovered in 2004.
- **Pontius Pilate** — confirmed by the Pilate Stone, discovered at Caesarea Maritima in 1961.
- **The ossuary of Caiaphas** — discovered in 1990 in Jerusalem.
- **The Gallio Inscription** — confirms the date of Paul's appearance before Gallio in Corinth (Acts 18:12).
- **Sir William Ramsay** — began his research as a skeptic, intending to disprove Acts, and concluded that "Luke is a historian of the first rank... this author should be placed along with the very greatest of historians" (*St. Paul the Traveller and the Roman Citizen*, 1895).

## 3. The Argument from the Origin of Christianity

**Formulation (N.T. Wright):**
1. The origin of Christianity requires historical explanation — how did this movement begin and survive?
2. The earliest Christians proclaimed the bodily resurrection of Jesus — a belief unprecedented in both Judaism and paganism.
3. No naturalistic explanation adequately accounts for the origin of this belief.
4. The best explanation is that Jesus actually rose from the dead.

**N.T. Wright's argument in detail (*The Resurrection of the Son of God*, 2003):**
- In Jewish theology, "resurrection" meant bodily rising at the end of time — corporate and eschatological. No one expected a single individual to rise before the end.
- In Greco-Roman thought, bodily resurrection was denied and even despised. The Greeks hoped for immortality of the soul, not resurrection of the body (cf. Acts 17:32 — the Athenians mocked Paul when he mentioned resurrection).
- Something caused the earliest Christians to proclaim — immediately, in Jerusalem, in the face of persecution — that one man had already been bodily raised from the dead. This represents a radical "mutation" in the concept of resurrection.
- The empty tomb alone would not produce this belief (it would suggest grave robbery). Visions alone would not produce it (they would suggest Jesus was in heaven as a spirit). Only the combination of the empty tomb AND genuine appearances of a bodily risen Jesus can account for the origin of Christianity.

## 4. Non-Christian Sources Confirming the Historical Jesus

### Tacitus (c. 56–120 AD)
*Annals* 15.44: "Christus, from whom the name had its origin, suffered the extreme penalty during the reign of Tiberius at the hands of one of our procurators, Pontius Pilate, and a most mischievous superstition, thus checked for the moment, again broke out not only in Judaea, the first source of the evil, but even in Rome."

### Josephus (c. 37–100 AD)
*Antiquities* 18.3.3 (the *Testimonium Flavianum*): Even in the reconstructed, stripped-down version (removing likely Christian interpolations), Josephus attests: Jesus was a wise teacher, had followers, was condemned by Pilate, and his followers continued after his death.

*Antiquities* 20.9.1: References "James, the brother of Jesus who is called the Christ" — confirming the existence of Jesus and his brother James.

### Pliny the Younger (c. 112 AD)
*Letters* 10.96: Reports to Emperor Trajan that Christians "were in the habit of meeting on a certain fixed day before it was light, when they sang in alternate verses a hymn to Christ, as to a god."

### Lucian of Samosata (c. 165 AD)
*The Death of Peregrinus*: Mocks Christians who "worship a man to this day — the distinguished personage who introduced their novel rites, and was crucified on that account."

### The Talmud (Sanhedrin 43a)
"On the eve of Passover Yeshu was hanged..." — confirms Jesus's execution at Passover, consistent with the Gospels.

### Mara bar Serapion (c. 73 AD)
A Syrian Stoic philosopher writes: "What advantage did the Jews gain from executing their wise King?... The wise King... lived on in the teaching which He had given."

**Defense Against "Retrofitting":**
- The Dead Sea Scrolls (discovered 1947-1956) contain copies of Isaiah, Psalms, and other prophetic books dated centuries before Christ — proving the prophecies existed before the events.
- The Septuagint (Greek OT translation, c. 250 BC) preserves the prophetic texts in a form predating Christianity.
- Many "fulfillments" (born in Bethlehem, betrayed for 30 silver pieces, crucifixion details) were beyond Jesus's voluntary control.

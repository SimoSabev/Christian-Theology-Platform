# Ontological Arguments — Deep Defense Library

> Arguments that deduce God's existence from the very concept of God — purely a priori reasoning from mainstream Trinitarian Christian philosophers.

---

## 1. Anselm's Classical Ontological Argument

**Core Formulation (*Proslogion*, ch. 2, 1078 AD):**
1. God is defined as "that than which nothing greater can be conceived" (*aliquid quo nihil maius cogitari possit*).
2. Even the fool (the atheist) understands this concept — it exists in the understanding.
3. It is greater to exist in reality than to exist only in the understanding.
4. If God exists only in the understanding, then a greater being can be conceived — one that also exists in reality.
5. But this contradicts the definition (a being greater than "that than which nothing greater can be conceived" is a contradiction).
6. Therefore, God must exist in reality, not merely in the understanding.

### Deep Defense

#### A. The Definition Is Not Arbitrary
Anselm's definition is not an arbitrary stipulation — it captures what theists *mean* by "God." Even atheists understand the concept: a being of unsurpassable greatness. The argument asks: given that we all understand this concept, what follows logically? Anselm addresses this to the atheist directly: you understand the concept, and understanding it commits you to its referent's existence.

**Anselm's own words:** "For even the fool is convinced that something than which nothing greater can be thought exists in the understanding, since when he hears this, he understands it; and whatever is understood exists in the understanding" (*Proslogion*, ch. 2).

#### B. Existence as a Great-Making Property
The claim that real existence is "greater" than merely mental existence is defensible: a real $100 can buy things; a merely imagined $100 cannot. A real God could create, sustain, and redeem; a merely conceived God could do nothing. Real existence adds causal power, actuality, and independence — all of which are plausibly "great-making."

#### C. Anselm's Response to Gaunilo
Gaunilo's "Lost Island" parody fails because:
- **Islands are contingent, material objects.** There is no intrinsic maximum for an island — one can always add another palm tree, another lagoon. The concept of "the greatest conceivable island" is incoherent because island-perfection has no logical maximum.
- **"Greatest conceivable being" is unique.** The properties of a maximally great being (omnipotence, omniscience, moral perfection) have logical maxima. You cannot be "more omnipotent" — omnipotence is already maximal.
- Anselm anticipated this: the argument applies only to "that than which nothing greater can be conceived," not to any arbitrary "greatest X."

**Key Defenders:**
- **Anselm of Canterbury** — *Proslogion* (1078), *Reply to Gaunilo*
- **Charles Hartshorne** — *The Logic of Perfection* (1962): Defended a modal version
- **Norman Malcolm** — "Anselm's Ontological Arguments" (1960): Argued *Proslogion* ch. 3 contains a distinct, valid argument

## 2. Descartes' Ontological Argument

**Core Formulation (*Meditations*, V, 1641):**
1. I have a clear and distinct idea of a supremely perfect being.
2. Existence is a perfection (a being lacking existence would not be supremely perfect).
3. Therefore, a supremely perfect being must exist.

### Deep Defense

#### A. The Analogy with Geometric Truths
Descartes compares God's existence to geometric truths: just as the essence of a triangle necessarily includes that its angles sum to 180°, the essence of a supremely perfect being necessarily includes existence. You cannot conceive of a triangle without three angles; you cannot conceive of a supremely perfect being without existence.

**Descartes' words:** "I cannot conceive of God except as existing, just as I cannot conceive of a mountain without a valley" (*Meditations*, V).

#### B. Leibniz's Improvement
**Gottfried Leibniz** recognized that Descartes' argument needs an additional premise: the concept of a supremely perfect being must be *possible* (logically consistent). Leibniz argued that all perfections are compatible because they are simple, positive, unlimited qualities — there can be no contradiction among them. Therefore, a supremely perfect being is possible. And if possible, by Descartes' reasoning, it is actual.

**Key Source:** Leibniz, "That a Most Perfect Being Exists" (1676).

## 3. Plantinga's Modal Ontological Argument

**Core Formulation (*The Nature of Necessity*, 1974):**
1. It is possible that a maximally great being (MGB) exists. [A maximally great being = a being that has maximal excellence (omnipotence, omniscience, moral perfection) in every possible world.]
2. If it is possible that an MGB exists, then an MGB exists in some possible world. [From the definition of possibility]
3. If an MGB exists in some possible world, then it exists in every possible world. [Because maximal greatness entails existence in every possible world — a being that exists contingently would not be maximally great]
4. If an MGB exists in every possible world, then it exists in the actual world.
5. Therefore, a maximally great being exists.

### Deep Defense

#### A. The Argument Is Logically Valid in S5
The argument is formally valid in modal logic system S5 (the standard system of modal logic accepted by most logicians). In S5, if something is possibly necessary, then it is necessary. Since maximal greatness includes necessary existence, the possibility of an MGB entails its actual existence.

#### B. Defense of the Key Premise: "It Is Possible That an MGB Exists"
This is the crucial premise. How do we know an MGB is possible?

**i. Conceptual Coherence:**
The concept of an MGB involves omnipotence, omniscience, and moral perfection. Each of these properties appears logically consistent. There is no known logical contradiction in the concept. As Plantinga argues: "The key premise — that maximal greatness is possibly exemplified — is not obviously false, and it seems to me to be true. It is certainly not demonstrably false" (*God, Freedom, and Evil*, 1977, p. 112).

**ii. No Demonstrated Incoherence:**
The burden of proof lies with the objector to demonstrate that the concept of an MGB is incoherent — i.e., involves a hidden logical contradiction. No such demonstration has been successful. The so-called paradoxes of omnipotence ("Can God create a stone so heavy He can't lift it?") are pseudo-paradoxes — they involve logical contradictions and thus do not count against omnipotence properly defined (omnipotence = the ability to do anything logically possible).

**iii. The Reverse Argument Fails:**
One might try to run the argument in reverse: "It is possible that an MGB does not exist → necessarily, an MGB does not exist." But this premise is not more plausible than Plantinga's premise. In fact, it is less plausible: we have positive reasons for thinking an MGB is possible (conceptual coherence, the success of other theistic arguments), whereas the denial amounts to asserting that maximal greatness is *impossible* — a very strong claim.

#### C. Plantinga's Own Assessment
Plantinga claims the argument shows that belief in God is *rational*: "It is rational to accept [the key premise]. It is therefore rational to accept the conclusion... This argument provides a good ground for rational belief in God" (*God, Freedom, and Evil*, 1977).

**Key Defenders:**
- **Alvin Plantinga** — *The Nature of Necessity* (1974), *God, Freedom, and Evil* (1977)
- **William Lane Craig** — Uses the argument as part of his cumulative case in debates
- **Robert Maydole** — "The Modal Perfection Argument" (2009): A strengthened version

## 4. The Argument from the Possibility of God (Robert Maydole)

**Formulation:**
1. If it is possible that a perfect being exists, then a perfect being exists.
2. A perfect being is possible (no logical contradiction can be derived from the concept).
3. Therefore, a perfect being exists.

Maydole strengthens the argument by proving step 2 through a systematic analysis showing that the properties constituting perfection (maximal power, knowledge, goodness) are logically compatible.

## 5. Gödel's Ontological Proof

**Kurt Gödel** (1906–1978), one of the greatest logicians in history, developed a formal ontological proof using modal logic and the theory of positive properties:

1. A property is "positive" if and only if its negation is not positive.
2. Any property entailed by a positive property is itself positive.
3. The property of being God-like is positive (where God-like = possessing all positive properties).
4. Necessarily, if something is God-like, it is God-like necessarily.
5. Therefore, necessarily, a God-like being exists.

**Defense:** Gödel's proof has been formalized and verified by computer (Christoph Benzmüller and Bruno Woltzenlogel Paleo, 2013). The logical structure is valid. The debate centers on the axioms about positive properties.

## Responding to Major Objections

### Objection 1: Kant — "Existence Is Not a Real Predicate"
**Immanuel Kant** (*Critique of Pure Reason*, 1781) argued that "existence" adds nothing to the concept of a thing: "A hundred real thalers do not contain the least coin more than a hundred possible thalers."

**Responses:**
1. **Plantinga's modal version avoids this entirely.** The key property is not "existence" but "necessary existence" — existing in every possible world. Necessary existence clearly adds something to a concept: a being that necessarily exists is greater than one that merely contingently exists.
2. **Kant's claim is questionable.** A real $100 bill can buy things; a merely possible $100 bill cannot. Existence does seem to add something — at minimum, causal power and actuality.
3. **Even granting Kant's point for the classical version,** it does not touch the modal version, which is the strongest form today.

### Objection 2: "The Argument Defines God into Existence"
**Response:** The argument does not define God into existence any more than geometry "defines" triangles into having 180° angle sums. It draws out what is logically entailed by the concept. If the concept of maximal greatness is coherent, then its instantiation follows necessarily. The objector must show the concept is incoherent — which has never been demonstrated.

### Objection 3: Multiple Maximally Great Beings
**Response:** There can be only one MGB. If there were two omnipotent beings, neither would be truly omnipotent (each would be limited by the other). Maximal greatness logically entails uniqueness.
